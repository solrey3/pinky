### 🪖 US Strikes Iran

On the morning of May 26, the US struck targets in southern Iran as Qatar-hosted ceasefire talks teetered. Key context:

- Trump had walked back his "largely negotiated" language on May 25, calling it "not finalized"
- Bolton called for scrapping the ceasefire entirely; Graham, Cruz, and WSJ editorial board skeptical
- Markets priced in the deal anyway — oil down another 4.58% Tuesday morning to $93.76
- Netanyahu's camp lobbying Trump to restart military strikes
- Russia simultaneously warning diplomats to evacuate Kyiv after the Oreshnik strikes

The Iran story is the most consequential geopolitical arc of the month: market pricing has run ahead of diplomatic reality at every step.

### 🎷 Sonny Rollins Dies at 95

Jazz's "Saxophone Colossus" died May 26. BrooklynVegan broke it; r/Music tribute posts poured in; the Morning Dispatch closed with: *"Go put on 'St. Thomas' or 'Tenor Madness' — right now, before you check another notification."*

Rollins was the last of the great bebop-to-hard bop saxophonists still living. His 1956 *Saxophone Colossus* album remains the definitive document of the tenor saxophone as a vehicle for simultaneous spontaneity and structure. His influence runs through every jazz musician who played after him and deep into hip-hop sampling culture.

## Synthesis Notes

The two editions mark the end of one sports era (Knicks returning to the Finals 27 years after their last trip) and the beginning of a geopolitical test (US striking Iran even as ceasefire talks proceeded). The market's response — ripping to all-time highs — reflects either supreme confidence that diplomacy will prevail or a terrifying disconnect between equity valuations and geopolitical risk. The AMD/NVDA divergence on Tuesday (AMD +17%, NVDA -2%) is a microcosm of the rotation underway in AI infrastructure: the market is no longer content with one monopoly narrative.

Sonny Rollins's death on a market ripping Tuesday is the kind of juxtaposition the newsletter was built for. Jazz built the architecture that hip-hop, soul, and modern music all inhabit — and the last great builder is gone.
