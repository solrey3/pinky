Strong comedy representation throughout (Community, Chappelle's Show, Newsradio, Parks & Rec, etc.).

## Key Observations

- **The Simpsons #1** — the long game; cultural DNA, not just a show
- **The Wire #2** — the great American crime drama; Baltimore as institutional critique
- **Mr. Robot #3** — cyberpunk/hacker realism; Elliot Alderson as unreliable narrator of the surveillance state; cross-references [[totalitarianism]] and [[predator]] concepts
- **Dragon Ball Z/Super #8** — only anime; energy/power-scaling aesthetics as a childhood formation
- **The Boondocks #9** — Aaron McGruder's political satire; the most subversive show in the top 10
- **Newsradio #16** — cult favorite; deeply underrated
- **Reality: Survivor #1** — the competitive game framework as legitimate TV craft; The Challenge and Big Brother round out a "strategic social game" sub-canon
- **No Netflix prestige dramas dominating** — The Crown, Stranger Things, Narcos are absent from the top tier; streaming hasn't displaced broadcast legacy

## Synthesis Notes

The TV canon shares DNA with the film and music canons — prestige and cultural weight matter, but so do personal joy (Newsradio, Community) and subversive craft (Boondocks, Mr. Robot). The Simpsons at #1 is a bold choice that resists the critical consensus (which would place The Wire #1) — it says something about the value of sustained comedic worldbuilding over peaked narrative drama.

The Wire's institutional critique resonates with political realism themes from [[the-prince]] and [[idealism-vs-realism]].
