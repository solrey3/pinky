# The Theory of Poker — Wisdom Extracted

**Author:** [[David Sklansky]]  
**Date:** 1987  
**Source:** [raw/books/The_Theory_of_Poker.md](../../raw/books/The_Theory_of_Poker.md)

## Summary

David Sklansky's *Theory of Poker* is the foundational text on the mathematical and strategic principles underlying all poker variations. Its central thesis — the Fundamental Theorem of Poker — states that every time you play a hand differently from how you would play it with perfect information, your opponents gain; every time they do the same, you gain. The book covers expected value, pot odds, implied odds, position, bluffing, semi-bluffing, game theory, and the psychological discipline required to execute mathematical strategy under pressure.

## Key Claims

1. **The Fundamental Theorem**: Play each hand as if you could see all opponents' cards; deviations from this are mistakes.
2. **Expected value drives everything**: Results-oriented thinking (judging decisions by outcomes) is the cardinal sin of poker.
3. **Position is king**: Acting last provides maximum information before committing chips — the most underrated advantage.
4. **Deception exists to cause mistakes**: Bluffing has no value except to make opponents play differently than they would with perfect information.
5. **Bankroll management ensures survival**: Proper bankroll sizing ensures survival through inevitable variance.

## Concepts Introduced

- [[Fundamental Theorem of Poker]] — The core principle that deviations from perfect-information play are mistakes
- [[Expected Value (EV)]] — The mathematical expectation of a decision, the proper metric for evaluation
- [[Pot Odds]] — Comparing bet size to total pot to determine mathematically correct calls
- [[Implied Odds]] — Estimating future bets you will win when completing a drawing hand
- [[Semi-Bluff]] — Combining fold equity with backup equity of improving
- [[Game Theory Optimal (GTO)]] — The baseline concept, though Sklansky focuses more on exploitative play
- [[Results-Oriented Thinking]] — The bias of judging decisions by outcomes rather than process

## Synthesis Notes

Sklansky's book is the theoretical foundation that [[Modern Poker Theory]] later operationalizes with solver-based GTO analysis. The Fundamental Theorem — play as if you know everything — is a poker-specific version of [[The Signal and the Noise]]'s Bayesian ideal: update beliefs with maximum possible information before acting. The book's emphasis on "mathematical expectation rather than short-term results" directly applies to [[Moneyball]]'s rejection of scout intuition in favor of statistical process. The warning against results-oriented thinking is the exact discipline that the MIT team in [[Bringing Down the House]] had to maintain: individual session outcomes are noise, the mathematical edge is signal. Sklansky's insight that "the value of deception decreases against weak opponents" is a strategic principle with broad application — misdirection only works against those paying attention.
