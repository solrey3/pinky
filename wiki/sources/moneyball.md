# Moneyball — Wisdom Extracted

**Author:** [[Michael Lewis]]  
**Date:** 2003  
**Source:** [raw/books/Moneyball.md](../../raw/books/Moneyball.md)

## Summary

Michael Lewis chronicles how the cash-strapped Oakland Athletics used sabermetrics — rigorous statistical analysis — to identify undervalued players and compete against wealthier teams. Led by GM Billy Beane, the A's discovered that baseball's conventional wisdom systematically overvalued metrics like batting average and stolen bases while undervaluing on-base percentage. The book is ultimately about market inefficiencies: any domain where collective judgment relies on tradition rather than evidence contains exploitable blind spots.

## Key Claims

1. **Market inefficiencies exist**: Baseball player valuation was systematically flawed because scouts judged "how players looked" rather than "what they produced."
2. **On-base percentage was undervalued**: Walks — boring and unglamorous — were among the most valuable offensive contributions, yet the market ignored them.
3. **Intellectual capital substitutes for financial capital**: Oakland could not match the Yankees' payroll but could out-analyze them.
4. **Tradition resists evidence**: Baseball's closed system reinforced biases for decades without external challenge.
5. **Innovation comes from outsiders**: Bill James pioneered sabermetrics as a night watchman; the A's exploited inefficiencies precisely because they were outsiders.

## Synthesis Notes

Moneyball is the practical demonstration of Silver's *Signal and the Noise* thesis: rigorous analysis defeats pundit intuition. It also extends [[The Wisdom of Crowds]] by showing that crowds (*i.e.*, the baseball market) can be wrong when independence and diversity are compromised by shared tradition. Beane's refusal to watch games — "emotional reactions to individual plays could distort rational long-term decision-making" — is a direct application of the Bayesian detachment Silver advocates. The book's core insight — that markets price assets based on narrative rather than evidence — connects to [[Bringing Down the House]], where MIT students exploited the casino industry's narrative-driven blindness to mathematical reality.
