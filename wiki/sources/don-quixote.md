## Summary

*Don Quixote* is widely considered the first modern novel and a foundational work of Western literature. The story follows Alonso Quijano, a middle-aged hidalgo from La Mancha who, after reading too many chivalric romances, adopts the identity of Don Quixote de la Mancha, a knight-errant sworn to revive chivalry. He recruits Sancho Panza, a simple farm labourer, as his squire. Together they embark on adventures that are equal parts comedy and tragedy, with Don Quixote seeing the world through the lens of romantic fantasy while Sancho brings earthy pragmatism. Over the two-part narrative — the second published ten years later with metafictional depth — the two characters gradually trade places: Don Quixote descends from illusion toward reality, while Sancho's spirit ascends from reality toward illusion. The novel was intended as a satire of chivalric romances but became something far larger: a meditation on perception, identity, aging, and the human need for meaning.

## Key Claims

1. **The novel is the first modern novel and one of the most influential works in world literature** — widely labeled "the best novel of all time" and the most translated book in history.
2. **The central engine is the dialectic between idealism and realism** — embodied by Don Quixote's romantic delusion and Sancho Panza's practical wit.
3. **The characters undergo a mutual transformation** — "Sancho's spirit ascends from reality to illusion, Don Quixote's declines from illusion to reality" (Madariaga).
4. **Metafiction is used deliberately and structurally** — Part Two includes characters who have read Part One, collapsing the boundary between story and reader.
5. **The work functions as both satire and sincere philosophy** — it mocks chivalric romances while affirming the dignity of pursuing noble ideals even in a mocking world.
6. **Perception is reality acting** — windmills become giants, inns become castles, proving that belief dictates the scale of our challenges.
7. **The transition from illusion to reality marks the tragic end of the life journey** — Don Quixote's renunciation of chivalry and subsequent death suggest that losing one's dreams is itself a form of dying.
