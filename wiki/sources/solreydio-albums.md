# SolReyDio — 150ish Favorite Albums

**Source:** [solrey3.com/solreydio](https://solrey3.com/solreydio/index.html)  
**Capture Date:** 2026-05-10  
**Curated by:** Sol Rey  
**Note:** Greatest-hits compilations excluded. All albums at least 3 years old.

## Summary

A personal canon of 178 albums ranked and annotated. The top 44 are explicitly ordered; the full list includes an additional 134 unranked entries. The curation is deeply personal and deliberately non-canonical in places — the author acknowledges rankings are "arbitrary and reductive" but constructs them anyway as a map of taste.

The list spans 1956–2024, with heavy concentration in four eras:
- **1965–1973:** The classic-rock/soul explosion (Beatles, Dylan, Stevie Wonder, Marvin Gaye)
- **Late 1980s–early 1990s:** Golden age hip-hop (Public Enemy, PE again, Eric B. & Rakim, NWA, De La Soul, Tribe, Nas, Biggie, Wu-Tang)
- **Mid-1990s:** Britpop/alt-rock peak (Pulp, Radiohead, Oasis, Blur)
- **2000s–2010s:** Indie/hip-hop crossover (OutKast, Kanye, Kendrick, St. Vincent)

Jazz, reggae, punk, classical, and electronic are represented but as satellites to the rock/hip-hop/soul core.

## Full List

178 total albums at least 3 years old, no greatest-hits compilations. The unranked entries extend the canon back to Frank Sinatra (1956) and forward to Kendrick Lamar's *GNX* (2024). Genre coverage: soul, jazz, classical, reggae, punk, metal, electronic, indie rock, alternative, and contemporary R&B.

## Patterns in the Canon

- **Public Enemy #1 + #27:** The only artist with two albums in the top 44. Hip-hop as political document is the highest value.
- **Radiohead ×3, OutKast ×3, Stevie Wonder ×4, The Beatles ×5, Kanye ×5, Kendrick ×5:** Repeat favorites suggest the curator rewards *oeuvre* depth over single-album impact.
- **Soundtracks and compilations:** Only *The Harder They Come*, *A Charlie Brown Christmas*, and *If I Were a Carpenter* appear — all are artist-curated works, not industry compilations.
- **No female solo artists in top 20:** Amy Winehouse (#34) and St. Vincent (#36) are the highest-ranked women. The absence is noted without commentary.

## Synthesis Notes

This list functions as a musical autobiography — every entry implies a moment of discovery. The heavy weighting toward 1990s hip-hop and Britpop suggests formative listening years in that window. The inclusion of *The Headphone Masterpiece* at #44 (a deeply obscure 2002 bedroom recording) signals that obscurity and rawness are valued over polish. The selection of *Different Class* at #4 over canonical British albums like *OK Computer* or *Nevermind* is the most idiosyncratic peak choice — suggesting a preference for wit and class consciousness over sonic grandeur.

The canon is self-aware about its own limitations: "arbitrary and reductive." That admission is the point. A ranked list of 178 albums is not a claim about objective quality; it's a hypothesis about what kinds of music matter to a particular listener at a particular moment. The SolReyDio project (solrey3.com/solreydio) is the public-facing version of this private map.
