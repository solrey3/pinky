# The Wisdom of Crowds — Wisdom Extracted

**Author:** [[James Surowiecki]]  
**Date:** 2004  
**Source:** [pinky/raw/books/The_Wisdom_of_Crowds.md](../raw/books/The_Wisdom_of_Crowds.md)

## Summary

Surowiecki demonstrates that large, diverse, independent groups consistently make better decisions than individual experts under the right conditions. The book identifies four key requirements: diversity of opinion, independence of thought, decentralization of knowledge, and a mechanism for aggregation (markets, voting, averaging). It also warns how social pressure, herding, and groupthink can turn smart crowds into dumb mobs.

## Key Claims

1. **Diversity > ability**: Diverse groups with independent thinking outperform homogeneous groups of experts.
2. **Four conditions for crowd wisdom**: Diversity, independence, decentralization, and aggregation are all necessary.
3. **Groupthink destroys wisdom**: Herding behavior, social pressure, and consensus-seeking erode the very independence that makes crowds smart.
4. **Markets as information processors**: Prediction markets and stock markets aggregate dispersed knowledge more effectively than central planning.
5. **Cognitive diversity matters more**: When solving complex problems, the variety of thinking styles within a group outweighs average individual IQ.

## Concepts Introduced

- [[Collective Intelligence]] — The emergent intelligence of properly structured groups
- [[Groupthink]] — The tendency for cohesive groups to suppress dissent and converge on poor decisions
- [[Information Cascade]] — When sequential decisions cause later actors to rationally ignore their own information
- [[Prediction Markets]] — Systems that aggregate forecasts through market mechanisms
- [[Cognitive Diversity]] — Diversity of thinking styles and problem-solving approaches, not just demographic diversity

## Synthesis Notes

Surowiecki's framework directly supports [[The Signal and the Noise]]'s finding that group forecasts outperform individual experts when properly aggregated. It also provides a modern epistemological counterpoint to [[Tao Te Ching]]'s organic trust in natural order — here, trust is placed in structured aggregation of independent human judgments. The book's warning about groupthink resonates with [[1984]]'s analysis of conformity, though Surowiecki offers a constructive rather than dystopian framing. The emphasis on market mechanisms as superior aggregators foreshadows [[Moneyball]]'s demonstration that markets (even flawed ones like baseball player valuation) contain exploitable inefficiencies when analyzed with rigor.
