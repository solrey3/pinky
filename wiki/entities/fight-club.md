# Fight Club

**Director:** David Fincher
**Genre:** Psychological thriller, Drama
**Runtime:** 139 min
**Country:** USA

## Summary

An unnamed insomniac narrator (Edward Norton) meets soap salesman Tyler Durden (Brad Pitt) on a flight and they start an underground fight club that becomes a terrorist organization. The narrator discovers he and Tyler are the same person. Based on Chuck Palahniuk's novel. The film was read as a men's liberation manifesto on release; it is more accurately a diagnosis of that pathology.

## Key Themes

- Consumerism and masculinity in crisis — 'the things you own end up owning you'
- The manufactured self: Tyler is a fantasy of pure will unencumbered by conscience
- Dissociation, insomnia, and alienation as symptoms of late capitalism
- Violence as false liberation — Project Mayhem reveals where the logic leads
- The unreliable narrator as a formal device for depicting fractured identity

## Why It Matters

Fincher's technical mastery is at its peak — the opening shot traveling through a neuron, the single-frame Tyler insertions, the IKEA catalog sequence. Pitt and Norton's chemistry is electric. The film was misread as macho catharsis on release; Fincher always intended it as a critique of exactly the people who loved it uncritically. Helena Bonham Carter's Marla is the film's moral compass, which most people miss.
