# Sonny Rollins

Theodore Walter "Sonny" Rollins (1930–2026) was an American jazz saxophonist widely regarded as one of the greatest improvisers in the history of the music. Known as the "Saxophone Colossus," he died on May 26, 2026, at 95. His 1956 album *Saxophone Colossus* remains the defining document of the tenor sax as a vehicle for spontaneity and structure in simultaneous tension. *St. Thomas*, the calypso-inflected opener, became one of jazz's most instantly recognizable melodies.

Rollins was a bebop-to-hard bop contemporary of Miles Davis, John Coltrane, and Thelonious Monk — and the last of that generation still living at the time of his death. His influence runs from jazz through soul and deep into hip-hop sampling culture. He was famous for extended solo sabbaticals (including practicing on the Williamsburg Bridge in the late 1950s) and for maintaining an improvisational standard that few players in any genre have approached.
