# Self-Reliance

The principle that individuals should trust their own intuition, perceptions, and genius over societal expectations, institutional authority, and collective consensus. Emerson's formulation is radical: "A foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds" — the self-reliant individual must be willing to contradict yesterday's self if today's intuition demands it.

## Notes

Self-reliance is both the most American and the most contested concept in this corpus. Emerson writes from a position of white male privilege in 1841, assuming the world will eventually recognize the nonconformist's genius. Ellison, writing in 1952, shows how systemic racism makes that recognition structurally impossible for the Black American — every attempt at self-expression is co-opted by institutions (the college, the Brotherhood, nationalism). The Taoist [[Wu Wei]] offers an alternative to both: rather than asserting the self (Emerson) or reacting to imposed identity (Ellison), it dissolves the boundary between self and natural order. And Virgil's [[Pietas]] is the direct opposite: the self must be sacrificed to destiny. These four poles — assertion, reaction, dissolution, and sacrifice — map the fundamental options for understanding the self's relationship to society.
