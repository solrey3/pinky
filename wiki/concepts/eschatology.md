# Eschatology

The study of "last things" — death, judgment, resurrection, and the ultimate destiny of humanity and the cosmos. Eschatological narratives provide frameworks for understanding history as teleological: moving toward a decisive endpoint that resolves all present suffering, conflict, and ambiguity.

## Notes

Both Revelation and the Aeneid use eschatological structure — present suffering justified by future glory — but with radically different tones. Virgil leaves the ending ambiguous: Aeneas's killing of Turnus is morally costly. Revelation offers more decisive closure: evil is destroyed, creation is renewed. Yet both require violence as the price of renewal. This connects to Machiavelli's [[Political Realism]]: founding requires sacrifice. The difference is theological — Revelation trusts divine justice to resolve the moral cost; Virgil leaves it unresolved; Machiavelli simply accepts it as the price of power.
