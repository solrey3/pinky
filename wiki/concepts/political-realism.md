# Political Realism

The view that politics operates under different ethical constraints than private morality. Effective statecraft requires pragmatic action that may violate conventional ethical standards — because maintaining order, security, and stability is itself a moral imperative that transcends individual virtue.

## Notes

Political realism creates the central tension with [[Tao Te Ching]]'s philosophy: Machiavelli demands strength and decisive action; Laozi counsels yielding and non-action. The Aeneid occupies a middle ground — Aeneas must act ruthlessly, but Virgil leaves the reader with moral ambiguity rather than Machiavelli's clear-eyed pragmatism. Orwell's [[1984]] shows what happens when political realism is taken to its totalitarian extreme: power becomes an end in itself, and all moral language is subordinated to it.
