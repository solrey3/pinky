# PARA

A universal organizational framework created by **Tiago Forte** for sorting digital information by **actionability** rather than by subject matter.

## The Four Categories

| Category | Definition | Time Horizon |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| **P**rojects | Discrete outcomes with deadlines | Weeks to months |
| **A**reas | Ongoing responsibilities without end | Indefinite |
| **R**esources | Reference material by topic | No expiration |
| **A**rchives | Inactive items from the other three | Closed |

## Core Insight

Most people sort information by type or topic (books, links, articles) which buries what matters *now*. PARA surfaces urgency: projects come first, areas second, resources third, archives last. The same four folders apply everywhere — notes app, file system, task manager, email.

## In Our 2nd Brain System

PARA governs the **raw-source lifecycle**, not the wiki structure:

| PARA Level | Where it lives | Role in our system |
|------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Projects** | Per-ingest batches, task manager | A reading sprint, a research phase, a month of journal entries |
| **Areas** | Fixed `raw/` subdirectories (`articles/`, `papers/`, etc.) | Ongoing streams you never stop adding to |
| **Resources** | `wiki/` — concepts, entities, sources, synthesis | Compiled, cross-linked knowledge base |
| **Archives** | Stale entries in `wiki/log.md`, ignored `raw/` files | Closed topics, completed research |

The wiki itself is **not** organized by PARA. It is organized by Zettelkasten linking principles (see [[Zettelkasten]]). PARA governs workflow; the Zettelkasten governs knowledge architecture.

## Notes

PARA is intentionally minimal. Even four folders is enough. It scales down to a single project and scales up to an enterprise. The key discipline is moving items to Archive when they are no longer active — without deleting them. Closure creates mental space.
