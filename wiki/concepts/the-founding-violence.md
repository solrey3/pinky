# The Founding Violence

The paradox that the establishment of political order, civilization, and empire typically requires acts of violence that the resulting order subsequently condemns. The founding violence is both necessary and morally ambiguous — it creates the peace it violates, and its memory must be either mythologized or suppressed for the new order to achieve legitimacy.

## Notes

The founding violence is the dark thread connecting Virgil, Machiavelli, and Revelation. All three acknowledge that creation requires destruction — whether of Turnus, of political rivals, or of the old heaven and earth. Where they differ is in how they resolve the moral cost. Machiavelli simply accepts it as necessary. Virgil leaves it unresolved — Aeneas hesitates before killing Turnus, and the poem ends with the soul fleeing to shadows rather than triumphant celebration. Revelation resolves it theologically: divine justice makes the violence righteous. These three resolutions — pragmatic acceptance, tragic ambiguity, and theological justification — map the Western tradition's options for thinking about political violence.
