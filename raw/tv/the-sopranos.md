# The Sopranos (1999–2007)

**Creator:** David Chase
**Network:** HBO
**Genre:** Crime drama
**Category:** Scripted Series
**Rank:** #6 (Scripted Series)

## Summary

New Jersey mob boss Tony Soprano (James Gandolfini) balances running a criminal organization with managing depression, anxiety, and a deeply dysfunctional family. He sees a therapist, Dr. Melfi (Lorraine Bracco). The show invented modern prestige television and everything that followed — complex antiheroes, cinematic direction, moral ambiguity — descended from it.

## Key Themes

- The American Dream as organized crime — Tony's aspirations are identical to any suburban father's
- Therapy and the limits of self-knowledge: Tony articulates his pathology without changing it
- The mob as a workplace — management problems, succession, labor-management conflict
- American masculinity and its performance: Tony's emotional life is literally violence
- The cut to black: the finale's refusal to give closure is a moral and aesthetic statement

## Why It Matters

The show that invented the modern prestige drama. Everything — the complex antihero, the cinematic production, the novelistic season arcs — traces back here. Gandolfini's Tony Soprano is one of the great performances in any medium: a man of genuine warmth and genuine violence, held in terrifying suspension. Chase's cut-to-black finale remains the most discussed and divisive ending in television history. The correct interpretation is that Tony is dead.
