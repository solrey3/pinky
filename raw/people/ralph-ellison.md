# Ralph Ellison

**Born:** 1914, Oklahoma City OK
**Nationality:** American
**Profession:** Novelist, essayist, scholar
**Active:** 1938–1994

## Summary

Invisible Man (1952) is the most important American novel of the 20th century. Ellison's argument — that a Black man in mid-century America is literally invisible to white society, which projects its own fantasies onto him rather than seeing him — is the most precise sociological observation in American fiction. His single novel is the most influential in the American canon.

## Key Works / Highlights

- Invisible Man (1952) — National Book Award; named the best American novel of the 20th century by a 1998 Radcliffe Institute poll
- The novel's central metaphor (invisibility as a social condition rather than a physical one) is the most precise description of racism's psychological mechanism
- His essays (Shadow and Act, Going to the Territory) are the most sophisticated criticism of American culture from a Black perspective
- His criticism of Mailer's 'The White Negro' is the most precise critical takedown in American literary history
- He spent 40 years on a second novel (Juneteenth, unfinished at his death) — the longest documented case of a masterwork's shadow preventing its successor

## Why They Matter

Invisible Man is the novel that named the experience of being unseen. Every writer who has come after Ellison writing about race in America has had to account for his work — either continuing it, arguing with it, or deliberately departing from it. His influence is that total.
