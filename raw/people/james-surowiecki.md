# James Surowiecki

**Born:** 1967, Connecticut
**Nationality:** American
**Profession:** Journalist, author
**Active:** 1994–present

## Summary

Author of The Wisdom of Crowds (2004), which systematically demonstrated that under the right conditions, aggregated group judgments outperform individual expert judgments. This has implications for prediction markets, polling, financial markets, and AI training. Longtime business and finance columnist for The New Yorker.

## Key Works / Highlights

- The Wisdom of Crowds (2004) — the foundational text for prediction markets, crowdsourcing, and democratic epistemology
- His research demonstrated the conditions under which crowds are wiser than experts (diversity of opinion, independence, decentralization, aggregation mechanism)
- His New Yorker column (2000–2017) was the most analytically rigorous regular business column in American journalism
- His counter-argument — when crowds become stupid (herding, information cascades) — is equally important to his central thesis
- The work directly influenced the design of prediction markets (Intrade, Polymarket, Metaculus)

## Why They Matter

Surowiecki's contribution is methodological — he documented the conditions under which collective intelligence works rather than simply claiming it does. The inverse (when crowds are stupid) is as important as the original claim. His work is the intellectual foundation of prediction markets.
