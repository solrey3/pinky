# Duke Ellington

**Born:** 1899, Washington DC
**Nationality:** American
**Profession:** Bandleader, composer, pianist
**Active:** 1923–1974

## Summary

The greatest composer in the history of jazz and one of the greatest in American music. Ellington led his orchestra continuously for over 50 years, composing more than 3,000 works including 'Mood Indigo,' 'Take the A Train,' and 'Sophisticated Lady.' His Ellington at Newport (1956) performance revitalized a career that had been commercially dormant for nearly a decade.

## Key Works / Highlights

- Ellington at Newport (1956) is the greatest concert comeback in jazz history — his 'Diminuendo and Crescendo in Blue' lasted 27 minutes and received a standing ovation
- Composed 3,000+ works including the American popular standard canon's most durable jazz compositions
- 50+ years of continuous orchestra leadership with the same identity — an unprecedented organizational achievement
- His long-term relationship with Billy Strayhorn (who wrote 'Take the A Train') is music's greatest creative partnership
- Presidential Medal of Freedom (1969); Pulitzer Prize (posthumous, 1999)

## Why They Matter

Ellington is the argument for jazz as a composer's medium rather than an improviser's medium — though he produced great improvisers as well. His ability to tailor compositions to specific musicians' strengths (Cootie Williams's plunger mute, Johnny Hodges's alto) invented the concept of band-as-instrument.
