# Otis Redding

**Born:** 1941, Dawson GA
**Nationality:** American
**Profession:** Singer, songwriter
**Active:** 1960–1967

## Summary

The king of Southern soul and the greatest live performer of his era. Redding's voice — raw, urgent, urgent — was the direct channel from gospel to rock 'n' roll that Sam Cooke had theorized and Redding fully realized. His Monterey Pop Festival performance (1967) was the first time a Black soul artist converted a white rock audience en masse. Died at 26.

## Key Works / Highlights

- Otis Blue/Otis Redding Sings Soul (1965) — the foundational document of Southern soul
- His Monterey Pop Festival performance (1967) is the greatest individual rock festival performance on record
- '(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay' (1968, posthumous) — his biggest commercial hit, released three days after his death in a plane crash
- His relationship with producer Steve Cropper and the MGs produced the Stax Records sound that defined Memphis soul
- He died at 26 in a plane crash, taking five members of the Bar-Kays with him — one of music's most devastating losses

## Why They Matter

Redding's death at 26 deprived soul music of its most complete voice at the exact moment he was crossing over to mainstream rock audiences. The recordings he left are sufficient to make the case — but the ones he didn't make are the permanent loss.
