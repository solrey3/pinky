# The Aviator's Wife (1981)

**Director:** Éric Rohmer
**Genre:** Drama, Romance
**Runtime:** 106 min
**Country:** France

## Summary

François (Philippe Marlaud), a young postal worker, is convinced his girlfriend Anne is still seeing her ex-boyfriend Christian, an aviator. He follows Christian through Paris and becomes entangled with a teenage girl, Lucie (Marie Rivière), who joins his surveillance. Misreadings and missed connections accumulate. The final revelation shows François has been wrong about everything. First in Rohmer's *Comedies and Proverbs* series.

## Key Themes

- Jealousy as epistemological distortion — what François 'sees' is what he fears, not what is
- The limits of observation: watching does not produce understanding
- Paris as a landscape of romantic misinterpretation
- The male ego's need to be the protagonist of someone else's story
- Youth, idleness, and the moral seriousness of small decisions

## Why It Matters

Rohmer's first Comedies and Proverbs entry. His filmmaking style — extended walking conversations, natural light, performers speaking at the pace of thought — was genuinely new and influenced a generation of European directors. The film is funny in the way that watching someone be confidently wrong is funny. The final image, where François understands he has wasted the day and possibly more, is quietly devastating.
