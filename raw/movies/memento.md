# Memento (2000)

**Director:** Christopher Nolan
**Genre:** Psychological thriller, Mystery
**Runtime:** 113 min
**Country:** USA

## Summary

Leonard Shelby (Guy Pearce) has anterograde amnesia — he cannot form new memories. He navigates his investigation into his wife's murder using Polaroid photos with notes and tattoos on his body. The film presents his story in reverse chronological order, placing the audience in his epistemological position: we know less than we think we know, and the ending is actually the beginning.

## Key Themes

- Memory as the foundation of identity and narrative — without it, who are you
- The unreliable narrator as structural form: the film's chronology is the argument
- Self-deception and the stories we need in order to function
- The detective genre inverted: the investigation is also concealing a truth
- Revenge and its futility when the avenger cannot retain the cause

## Why It Matters

Nolan's breakout film, made for $9M. The reverse chronology isn't a gimmick — it's the only correct way to tell the story, because it puts us in Leonard's cognitive position. Guy Pearce is superb. Joe Pantoliano as Teddy is the film's moral center, which the structure slowly reveals. The final revelation is one of cinema's great gut punches: we understand why Leonard chooses not to know.
