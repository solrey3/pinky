# Rushmore (1998)

**Director:** Wes Anderson
**Genre:** Comedy-drama
**Runtime:** 93 min
**Country:** USA

## Summary

Max Fischer (Jason Schwartzman) is an academically failing but extracurricularly obsessive student at Rushmore Academy who falls for a first-grade teacher (Olivia Williams) and forms a strange friendship with industrialist Herman Blume (Bill Murray). A love triangle between Max, Blume, and Ms. Cross descends into elaborate theatrical warfare — and, eventually, a mutual recognition of shared loneliness.

## Key Themes

- Ambition without discipline: Max's plays are masterpieces, his grades are catastrophic
- Class anxiety — Max is on scholarship, keenly aware of his outsider status among the wealthy
- Grief and substitution: Max's mother died; his extracurriculars are a monument to her
- The mentor-protégé dynamic as mutual rescue: Blume and Max save each other
- Theater as control — staging reality because reality is uncontrollable

## Why It Matters

The film that defined Wes Anderson's aesthetic and launched Bill Murray's late-career renaissance as a melancholy icon. Mark Mothersbaugh's score + British Invasion soundtrack (The Faces, Cat Stevens, The Kinks) sets the tone. Schwartzman is a revelation. Anderson's visual precision — symmetry, overhead shots, tableau staging — arrives fully formed here. One of the great films about the performance of ambition.
