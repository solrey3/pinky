# Glengarry Glen Ross (1992)

**Director:** James Foley
**Genre:** Drama
**Runtime:** 100 min
**Country:** USA

## Summary

A real estate sales office in Chicago. The salesmen — Shelley Levene (Jack Lemmon), Ricky Roma (Al Pacino), Dave Moss (Ed Harris), George Aaronow (Alan Arkin) — are told by Blake (Alec Baldwin) that the top two producers keep their jobs; the rest are fired. A burglary occurs. The film is David Mamet's screenplay of his own play, translated to screen with its language, rhythm, and ethics intact.

## Key Themes

- Capitalism's reduction of human value to production metrics
- Language as power and manipulation — every conversation is a transaction
- Masculinity and performance under economic pressure
- Complicity: every character compromises for survival
- The American sales culture as a theater of desperation and dignity

## Why It Matters

'Always be closing.' Baldwin's Blake monologue is one of the most terrifying scenes in American film — eight minutes of pure power dynamics. Mamet's dialogue rhythm (the overlaps, the repetitions, the non-sequiturs) is a specific music. Pacino is at his most controlled; Lemmon's Levene is one of cinema's great portraits of a man past his prime who cannot accept it. The film refuses sympathy without refusing humanity.
