# Brewster's Millions (1985)

**Director:** Walter Hill
**Genre:** Comedy
**Runtime:** 97 min
**Country:** USA

## Summary

Minor league baseball pitcher Monty Brewster (Richard Pryor) must spend $30 million in 30 days without telling anyone why — without owning anything, making charitable donations above a set limit, or destroying the money — to inherit $300 million from a dead uncle. The catch: every scheme to lose money makes him more popular, more respected, and more successful. Spike Lee and John Candy co-star.

## Key Themes

- Paradox of wealth: trying to waste money creates more value than spending it
- The performative nature of consumption — what money means vs. what it does
- Capitalism's absurdity: Brewster's 'None of the Above' mayoral campaign resonates
- Friendship under financial pressure (Spike Lee's 'Spike')
- The self-defeating nature of trying to fail — competence reasserts itself

## Why It Matters

Richard Pryor's warmth and comic timing make this premise work. The 'None of the Above' political campaign is more pointed satire than the film gets credit for. The logic puzzle at the film's core — how to spend $30M without keeping anything — is genuinely clever and drives inventive set pieces. A film that reveals what money is actually for.
